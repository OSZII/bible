# Bible Translation Research

Updated: 2026-05-20

## Scope

This note is for Orthodox-first Bible translation choices in the project. The safest baseline is an edition with clear Orthodox ecclesial authority. Where that does not exist in a modern target language, the next best choice is:

- Old Testament: Septuagint/LXX-based text or a Catholic/interconfessional Bible with the wider deuterocanonical corpus, clearly labelled as a fallback.
- New Testament: Byzantine/Patriarchal-text alignment where available, otherwise a mainstream reader translation clearly labelled as non-Orthodox fallback.

Do not label Catholic, Protestant, or interconfessional editions as `Orthodox` unless they are actually Orthodox-produced or Orthodox-authorized.

## Serbian Findings

The Serbian Orthodox Church's Synodal New Testament has strong authority. Radomir Rakic's article on the Synodal translation says the Holy Synod approved the translation in 1984 as a Church-authorized translation, and later explains that the Holy Assembly of Bishops promulgated it as an authentic translation usable in church services and liturgical rites.

The full Serbian Holy Synod Bible is more complete than the older Danicic-Karadzic pairing because it includes the deuterocanonical books. The Serbian Patriarchate shop lists a Synodal Bible issued by the Holy Synod of Bishops of the Serbian Orthodox Church, with Danicic for the Old Testament, Amfilohije/Atanasije for the deuterocanonical books, and the Holy Synod Commission for the New Testament. Svetosavlje lists the same structure for the 2014 printed edition.

The older Danicic-Karadzic Bible remains historically important and still circulates. Rakic notes that the older Danicic Old Testament plus Vuk Karadzic New Testament combination had long been published together, and that some people and communities still prefer Vuk's New Testament for its language. For this project, though, the Synodal/SPC edition is the better Orthodox recommendation because it includes the Synodal New Testament and the wider Orthodox canon.

## German Findings

German-speaking Orthodoxy does not appear to have one universally received complete Bible translation.

The Orthodox School Office in Austria describes the Orthodoxe Schulbibel as the first Orthodox school Bible in the whole German-speaking area. It includes the four Gospels, Acts, and selected Psalms, and its biblical text was revised according to the Byzantine text. The Orthodox School Office's history page adds that it was published in 2015 for Orthodox religious instruction and that the text started from existing translations and was revised according to the Byzantine text.

For the New Testament and Psalter, Edition Hagia Sophia lists an Orthodox German edition containing the complete New Testament, Orthodox Psalter, Psalm 151, and the nine biblical Odes. The publisher says its basis is the Greek Patriarchal text of Constantinople used in the Orthodox Church's worship, with translation work by S'chi-Archimandrit Justin Rauer and collaborators.

For the Old Testament, Septuaginta Deutsch is relevant because it is the first complete German translation of the Septuagint. The German Bible Society says it is a philological, readable translation of the Greek text. Its shop page says it made the Old Testament available to Orthodox communities in the German-speaking area in a common language. This makes it useful for study/reference, but a Russian Orthodox parish article from Dresden says its closeness to the source syntax makes it less suitable for liturgy.

Lutherbibel has some practical Orthodox use, but it should be treated as a fallback/common-language option, not as an Orthodox translation. The Dresden Russian Orthodox parish article says German-speaking Russian Orthodox communities often use common German translations for Scripture readings and that this is not rarely Lutherbibel. The same article also flags Orthodox concerns: Luther's Old Testament starts from the Masoretic text and lacks the Orthodox Septuagint baseline.

Schlachter does not look like the right Orthodox answer. The Austrian Bible Society describes Schlachter as a translation by Swiss preacher Franz Eugen Schlachter; it uses Hebrew/Greek source-text traditions and contains the Old Testament without late writings/deuterocanonical books. That canon profile is the main reason not to choose it as an Orthodox default.

## Spanish Findings

I did not find a properly edited and published Orthodox Bible in Spanish. An Antiochian Orthodox article in Spanish explicitly says that neither Spanish nor French has a proper Orthodox Bible edition, while explaining the Orthodox reliance on the Septuagint and the wider Orthodox Old Testament canon.

For Orthodox alignment, the strongest Spanish direction is Septuagint-based. The older Juenemann Bible is a Catholic Spanish translation from the Greek Septuagint line and is useful because it is LXX-oriented, but it should not be labelled Orthodox. The modern La Biblia griega Septuaginta project from Ediciones Sigueme / Verbo Divino is a scholarly Spanish translation of the Septuagint planned in four Old Testament volumes. It is valuable as a reference source, but licensing and completeness need to be checked before using it as a project text.

For a complete readable Spanish fallback, use a Catholic or interconfessional Bible with deuterocanonical books and clearly label it as a fallback. Dios Habla Hoy with deuterocanonicals is available from Bible Society channels, but it is not Orthodox and does not solve the full Orthodox canon/source-text issue.

Recommended label: `Spanish LXX-oriented fallback / Juenemann or modern Septuagint reference`.

## Italian Findings

I did not find a complete Orthodox Italian Bible standard. The practical reader baseline is the CEI 2008 Bible because it is the official Catholic Italian translation and includes the Catholic deuterocanonical core. That makes it better than Protestant Italian editions for canon coverage, but it is still not Orthodox.

For Orthodox Old Testament alignment, use Italian Septuagint material as reference/additions. La Bibbia dei Settanta, edited in the Italian scholarly tradition and available with Greek text and Italian translation, is the relevant direction for LXX comparison. This matches the broader Orthodox textual criterion used elsewhere in this document: the Septuagint is the Old Testament baseline for Orthodox alignment.

Recommended label: `Italian CEI Catholic core + Septuagint reference/additions`.

## French Findings

I did not find a proper Orthodox French Bible. The same Antiochian Orthodox Spanish article says French has no proper Orthodox Bible edition, while pointing to Pierre Giguet's French Septuagint as valuable.

The best practical complete French fallback is TOB 2010. Orthodox-facing French booksellers and the French Bible Society describe TOB 2010 as adding six Orthodox-used books: 3 and 4 Esdras, 3 and 4 Maccabees, Prayer of Manasseh, and Psalm 151. This still does not make TOB an Orthodox translation, but it makes it much closer to project needs than a Protestant or standard Catholic-only canon.

For Septuagint reference, La Bible d'Alexandrie is important. Editions du Cerf describes it as a French translation/commentary of the Greek Septuagint, using the Rahlfs Septuagint text; the Pentateuch volume notes the Septuagint as the biblical text used in the Orthodox world. It is academic/reference material rather than a simple project-reader Bible.

Recommended label: `French TOB 2010 reader fallback + Septuagint reference`.

## Greek Findings

Greek is the strongest case because the Orthodox textual baseline is Greek itself.

For the Old Testament, use the Septuagint. Septuagint.Bible, a joint project of the Greek Orthodox Archdiocese of America and the Hellenic Bible Society, states its aim is to make available the living Septuagint text as used in Orthodox public worship and private devotion and to publish a properly edited edition for divine service.

For the New Testament, use the 1904 Patriarchal / Antoniades text. Apostoliki Diakonia describes the 1904 Antoniades critical edition as the critical edition of the text of the Orthodox Eastern Church, prepared from the manuscript tradition and the lectionary usage of Orthodox churches.

Recommended label: `Greek Septuagint OT + Patriarchal 1904 NT`.

## Georgian Findings

Georgian has a deep Orthodox Bible tradition. A Bible Translator article says Georgian Bible translation has a history of more than fifteen centuries and that St. Giorgi Mtatsmindeli's recension of the New Testament and Psalms became canonical for the Georgian Orthodox Church.

For a modern whole-Bible Georgian project source, the key Orthodox baseline is the 1989 Georgian Orthodox Church / Patriarchy edition. The same article identifies the 1989 Bible as a new translation into modern Georgian published by the Georgian Orthodox Church, also called the Patriarchy edition. It also notes that the Patriarchate's Bible translation group continued publishing books such as Matthew, Mark, Luke, and Genesis in the 2000s.

The Institute for Bible Translation also has a complete modern Georgian Bible from 2002. That is useful for comparison/readability, but it should not replace the Georgian Orthodox Patriarchy edition as the Orthodox-first recommendation.

Recommended label: `Georgian Orthodox Patriarchy edition`.

## Russian Findings

The Russian Synodal Translation is the standard Russian reader/home-study Bible for Orthodox contexts, even though Church Slavonic remains the liturgical baseline. Russian Orthodox and Bible-translation sources describe the Synodal Translation as a nineteenth-century translation completed in 1876 and connected with the Holy Synod.

Important caveat: the Russian Synodal Old Testament is not a pure Septuagint translation. Orthodox sources note that it used the Hebrew/Masoretic text in a way that differed from the pure Greek/Slavonic Orthodox textual line, while retaining traditional Orthodox book names/order and some Septuagint/Church Slavonic influence. For this project, that means the Synodal Translation is the practical Russian default, but it should be labelled as Synodal/reader Russian, not as a pure LXX Russian OT.

Recommended label: `Russian Synodal Translation / reader Orthodox standard`.

## Concrete Local Source Plan

For the Serbian Bible I will use these sources:

- Old Testament protocanonical books: Djura Danicic.
- Old Testament deuterocanonical books: Metropolitan Amfilohije Radovic and Bishop Atanasije Jevtic, following the full Serbian Holy Synod/SPC edition.
- New Testament: Commission of the Holy Synod of Bishops of the Serbian Orthodox Church.
- Label in the project: `Serbian Synodal / Holy Synod SPC`.

For the German Bible I will use these sources:

- Base complete German Bible: Einheitsuebersetzung 2016 for the 73-book Catholic core, including the Catholic deuterocanonical books.
- Orthodox additions: Septuaginta Deutsch for the Septuagint-based Orthodox additions and reference material, especially 1 Esdras, Prayer of Manasseh, Psalm 151, 3 Maccabees, and 4 Maccabees where kept by the project canon.
- Broader appendix material: a German 4 Ezra / 2 Esdras source from the Vulgate-apocrypha tradition, such as Hermann Gunkel's German 4 Esra translation, if the current project canon keeps 2 Esdras.
- Label in the project: `German Catholic core + Orthodox Septuagint additions`.

For Spanish I would use:

- Orthodox-aligned reference: Juenemann or the modern Spanish Septuagint project, subject to licensing/completeness.
- Reader fallback: a Spanish Catholic/interconfessional Bible with deuterocanonical books if a complete readable text is needed before a stronger LXX source is licensed.
- Label in the project: `Spanish LXX-oriented fallback / Juenemann or modern Septuagint reference`.

For Italian I would use:

- Base complete Italian Bible: CEI 2008 for the Catholic core.
- Orthodox additions/reference: Italian Septuagint material such as La Bibbia dei Settanta where licensing permits.
- Label in the project: `Italian CEI Catholic core + Septuagint reference/additions`.

For French I would use:

- Base complete French Bible: TOB 2010 because it includes the extra Orthodox-used books added in that edition.
- Orthodox reference: La Bible d'Alexandrie or Pierre Giguet's Septuagint for LXX comparison.
- Label in the project: `French TOB 2010 reader fallback + Septuagint reference`.

For Greek I would use:

- Old Testament: Greek Septuagint.
- New Testament: 1904 Patriarchal / Antoniades text.
- Label in the project: `Greek Septuagint OT + Patriarchal 1904 NT`.

For Georgian I would use:

- Base complete Georgian Bible: Georgian Orthodox Church / Patriarchy edition, 1989.
- Comparison only: Institute for Bible Translation modern Georgian Bible, 2002.
- Label in the project: `Georgian Orthodox Patriarchy edition`.

For Russian I would use:

- Base complete Russian Bible: Russian Synodal Translation, 1876 line.
- Liturgical reference only if needed: Church Slavonic / Elizabeth Bible tradition.
- Label in the project: `Russian Synodal Translation / reader Orthodox standard`.
